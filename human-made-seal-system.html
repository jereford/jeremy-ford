Human Made: a system to distinguish and celebrate art created by people

Human Made is a seal system that helps content creators indicate their image, video, or text was made without the use of AI.

A microsite for creators to download assets and view usage recommendations.

info

According to a 2025 Pew Research Center survey: "76% of Americans say it is extremely or very important to be able to tell if pictures, videos, or text were made by AI. And 53% of Americans are not too or not at all confident they can detect if something is made by AI versus a person.”

Concept

With the recent surge of AI applications that generate high-quality visuals, many of us find it challenging to differentiate between output that's been created by a human being and output that hasn't. This is problematic. Studies show that people care about who (or what) created the thing they're looking at.

One night, before going to sleep (yes, these kinds of issues keep me awake), I sketched ideas for a seal that would certify the authenticity of human-made content. I saw it as the equivalent of the 'USDA Organic' or 'B Corp' seals that instantly communicate integrity and instill confidence. I wasn't sure what to do with my ideas until I read a LinkedIn post from Creative Director Alex Fuller. Her post included a link to a BBC article featuring several 'AI-free' logos designed by artists from all over the world. The existing 'AI-free' logos were great, but didn't quite hit the mark for me. I wanted something that felt more human. I was inspired to refine one of my sketches and share the progress on LinkedIn as a follow-up to Alex's post. I shared my proposal for a Human Made seal and it received a considerable amount of engagement. This validated the need for a usable seal and motivated me to push forward.

My original LinkedIn post that proposed a Human Made seal people could embed in their work.

Seal design

The icon element of the logo was hand-drawn in Figma, over my original sketch that I drew with pencil and paper. The text element of the logo was created by customizing Gaegu, a handwritten-style font designed by JIKJI SOFT, a Korean type foundry. The shapes and colors are intended to represent and celebrate the unique building blocks of human creation: blood, sweat, tears, and love.

Microsite

Using Figma, I published an interactive microsite, as a Figma prototype, that allows creators to download different versions of the seal to suit varyious contexts. The site also includes recommendations that creators can browse to understand how to apply the seals to their work: sizing, placement, and alignment.

It's worth mentioning a potential risk of the project: there is nothing restricting the use of the seal. This means that the seal could be applied to AI-generated content. Human Made operates on the honor system; a creator pledges what they created was indeed conceived and created with no assistance from AI.

What's next?

Lots of questions remain about the future of this system (or any certification system) remain:

  • How might we prevent a seal like this one from being misused/applied to AI-generated content?
  • Is there an acceptable percentage of AI assistance that's acceptable? For example, if an author writes an article themselves and uses Grammarly to edit their piece is it considered human-made? There are nuances to consider.
  • How might we empower content creators to quickly and easily embed the seal in their work? Perhaps there's a tool that can automatically embed the seal instead of creators having to manually affix it to every artifact.
  • Who decides what system/seal becomes the standard?
  • What about audio? Human-created music and podcasts also need to be differentiated from AI-generated content.

Progress!

As AI technology evolves at a rapid pace, as do tools to detect its use. A few emerging solutions to address the content authenticity issue that I've encountered after publishing my microsite: LinkedIn utilizes Content Credentials to indicate if an image was generated using AI, Google created SynthID to detect if an image was generated using Google Gemini, and GPTZero has a browser extension to detect AI usage in blocks of text.
