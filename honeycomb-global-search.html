That process that brought Honeycomb's global search feature from conception to general accessibility (GA) involved a moonshot, Hack Week, unmoderated user research, team implementation, and measurement.

The moonshot

"Moonshots" at Honeycomb are conceptual prototypes intended to address familiar customer pains associated with an off-roadmap feature. Product Designers are encouraged to “shoot for the moon” by designing their ideal solution to a problem, unbound by scope, technical limitations, or timelines. Designers benefit from moonshots by having space to think creatively and holistically about a problem they’re passionate about. The business benefits when moonshots influence the product roadmap, which happens quite often – when an idea is so undeniably valuable that it’s prioritized and resourced to bring it to life.

Shortly after starting at Honeycomb in 2020, I worked on a moonshot for Global Search, which I presented at a company-wide kick-off meeting. The goal of the project was to address long-standing feedback from users who found it difficult to locate important objects within their team’s instance. An inherent consequence of Honeycomb being an immensely powerful observability product is that it has a deep informational hierarchy. This hierarchy includes a plethora of object types: queries, public and private boards, triggers, SLOs, traces, spans, datasets, and derived columns. This can sometimes make it hard to locate a specific thing. The theory was that a search function would alleviate most of our users’ frustrations associated with this problem.

Roadmap

During Hack Week, a team implemented a light version of the moonshot and first place of 10 teams. Their prize: consideration for inclusion in the upcoming roadmap!

Sure enough, the Global Search feature made its way onto our roadmap the following quarter, and back into my hands. As part of a dedicated team for the project, we would carry the torch over the finish line by adapting the winning Hackathon project into the product. The team was given about two months to build and ship the feature. Given the tight timeline, we assessed effort and impact, and landed on the following acceptance criteria (the majority of which we were able to leverage from Hack Week, when a team implemented a light version of the moonshot in alpha):

  • Accessible from anywhere (global)
  • Summoned via keyboard shortcut (cmd+k or ctrl+k)
  • Results to display in a modal vs a full page to feel more lightweight and not navigate users away from their workflwow
  • Supported object types: public boards, datasets, named queries, triggers, SLOs, derived columns
  • Leverage Elastic Search to allow users to search for almost anything in their instance from an elegant modal window
  • A filter to allow the user to quickly narrow down their results by object type.

We decided the following could be reserved for a future iteration (out of scope):

  • Suggested searches
  • Recent searches
  • Moving recent activity and recent boards from the navigation into the search modal
  • A delightful empty state (something other than simple text)

Now to the winner: Global Search! Find anything from a single search bar: queries, SLOs, triggers... you name it. Validation

Now that we knew Global Search was something we were going to put in front of users, we wanted to make sure the experience built during Hack Week would meet expectations and be usable. We decided to send a survey to Honeycomb users in our Pollinators Slack channel to answer some burning questions that would influence a few decisions. [explain pollinators]. The survey looked like this:

  • Where would you expect to click to open a global search?
  • What keyboard shortcut would you expect to use to launch a global search?
  • Where would you expect global search results to show up? Modal / Side panel / Header / Other
  • What kinds of things would you want to search for?
  • What kinds of things would you search for most often?
  • For the things you would search for most often, what metadata would you expect to see?
  • What metadata in a search result would most help you identify whether that's the thing you were looking for?
  • In order to narrow your search, would you prefer to: Specify the type of thing before searching, Filter the type of thing after searching, Both, Not interested in narrowing my search?
